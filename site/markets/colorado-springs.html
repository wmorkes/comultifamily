The Colorado Springs Multifamily Market

Colorado Springs is one of Colorado's most fundamentally sound multifamily markets. Five military installations — Fort Carson, Peterson Space Force Base, Schriever Space Force Base, Cheyenne Mountain Space Force Station, and the United States Air Force Academy — create a stable, recession-resistant renter base that is largely immune to the economic cycles that impact civilian employment-driven markets. Military households represent a disproportionately reliable segment of the renter population, with consistent income, predictable rotation cycles, and strong payment histories.

Beyond the military anchor, Colorado Springs has undergone a meaningful economic diversification over the past decade. The cybersecurity and defense technology sectors have grown substantially, driven in part by the concentration of Space Force and cyber mission units. UCHealth and Centura Health anchor a large healthcare employment base. Tourism, retail, and outdoor recreation continue to attract young renters who choose Colorado Springs for lifestyle reasons and stay for employment opportunity.

From an investment perspective, Colorado Springs offers a compelling value proposition relative to Denver. Cap rates in the Springs have historically run 50–100 basis points wider than comparable Denver product — a spread that has attracted a growing cohort of institutional buyers who recognize the market's stability and yield characteristics. We've been active in this market for over a decade and have closed some of the highest per-unit prices ever recorded for pre-1990 vintage construction here.

What We Look For in Colorado Springs Assets

Value-add assets in the Springs typically offer renovation upside that can be executed at lower cost than Denver, given the market's more favorable construction labor environment. Submarkets near Fort Carson on the south side of the city and near Peterson on the east side tend to carry the strongest military renter demand. The north end of the city near the Air Force Academy and Interquest attracts a more affluent renter profile with strong income demographics.

Affordable housing in Colorado Springs represents an underdiscovered investment category. The city has a significant affordable housing need relative to its income demographics, and LIHTC and Section 8 assets here have attracted strong non-profit and institutional buyer interest. We've closed multiple affordable transactions in the market and maintain an active buyer list for future opportunities.

New supply has been more measured in Colorado Springs than in Denver, which has helped the market avoid the severe vacancy pressure that impacted some Denver submarkets in 2023–2025. As the Denver pipeline has thinned, we've seen buyers increasingly look south for yield without sacrificing market quality — a trend we expect to continue.
