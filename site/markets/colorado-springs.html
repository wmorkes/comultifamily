The Colorado Springs Multifamily Market

Colorado Springs is one of Colorado's most fundamentally sound multifamily markets. Five military installations — Fort Carson, Peterson Space Force Base, Schriever Space Force Base, Cheyenne Mountain Space Force Station, and the United States Air Force Academy — create a stable, recession-resistant renter base that is largely immune to the economic cycles that impact civilian employment-driven markets. Military households represent a disproportionately reliable segment of the renter population, with consistent income and strong payment histories.

Beyond the military anchor, Colorado Springs has undergone a meaningful economic diversification over the past decade. The cybersecurity and defense technology sectors have grown substantially, driven in part by the concentration of Space Force and cyber mission units. UCHealth and Centura Health anchor a large healthcare employment base. Tourism, retail, and outdoor recreation continue to attract young renters who choose Colorado Springs for lifestyle reasons and stay for employment opportunity.

From an investment perspective, Colorado Springs offers a compelling value proposition relative to Denver. Cap rates in the Springs have historically run 50–100 basis points wider than comparable Denver product — a spread that has attracted a growing cohort of institutional buyers who recognize the market's stability and yield characteristics. We've been active in this market for over a decade and have closed some of the highest per-unit prices ever recorded for pre-1990 vintage construction here.

What We Look For in Colorado Springs Assets

Value-add assets in the Springs typically offer renovation upside that can be executed at lower cost than Denver, given the market's more favorable construction labor environment. Submarkets near Fort Carson on the south side of the city and near Peterson on the east side tend to carry the strongest military renter demand. The north end of the city near the Air Force Academy and Interquest attracts a more affluent renter profile with strong income demographics.

Affordable housing in Colorado Springs represents an underdiscovered investment category. The city has a significant affordable housing need relative to its income demographics, and LIHTC and Section 8 assets here have attracted strong non-profit and institutional buyer interest. We've closed multiple affordable transactions in the market and maintain an active buyer list for future opportunities.

New supply has been more measured in Colorado Springs than in Denver, which has helped the market avoid the severe vacancy pressure that impacted some Denver submarkets in 2023–2025. As the Denver pipeline has thinned, we've seen buyers increasingly look south for yield without sacrificing market quality — a trend we expect to continue.

Colorado Springs Submarkets

Downtown / Midtown
Downtown Colorado Springs, Old Colorado City, Manitou Springs, Cascade Ave, Nevada Ave corridor

The urban core of Colorado Springs is attracting renewed investment as the city's downtown revitalization has accelerated. Walkable retail, the arts district, and proximity to Garden of the Gods and Pikes Peak attract a lifestyle-oriented renter who values amenities over square footage. New Class A development has absorbed well here, and the buyer profile skews toward urban-core specialists and regional private equity.

North / Briargate / Interquest
Briargate, Interquest Parkway, Northgate, Air Force Academy (south gate), Chapel Hills

The most affluent submarket in Colorado Springs, anchored by Interquest's retail and employment corridor and proximity to the Air Force Academy. Household incomes here support Class A rents, and the submarket has attracted institutional buyers seeking the Springs' yield premium without sacrificing renter quality. USAA's regional campus and strong healthcare employment anchor the professional renter base.

East / Powers Corridor
Powers Boulevard, Peterson Space Force Base, Stetson Hills, Falcon, Cimarron Hills

The Powers corridor is the fastest-growing submarket in Colorado Springs, driven by Peterson Space Force Base's expansion and significant retail and logistics employment. Military households represent a disproportionate share of renters here, creating the stable, predictable occupancy that institutional buyers underwrite as recession-resistant. Value-add product on the east side has attracted national private equity buyers seeking basis and yield.

South / Fort Carson
Fort Carson corridor, Security-Widefield, Fountain, Stratmoor Hills, South Academy

Fort Carson — one of the largest Army installations in the country — anchors demand throughout the south side of Colorado Springs. Military families and support personnel create a resilient workforce renter base with strong payment histories and predictable rotation cycles. Cap rates in this submarket tend toward the higher end of the Colorado Springs range, offering a yield premium in exchange for a buyer pool that specializes in military-adjacent housing.

Monument / North Douglas County
Monument, Palmer Lake, Castle Rock (south), Woodmoor

The northern fringe of the Colorado Springs MSA benefits from dual commuter access to both the Springs and the Denver metro via I-25, attracting a higher-income renter profile than most of the market. New construction in Monument has demonstrated strong absorption from relocating households and remote workers. This submarket commands among the tightest cap rates in the MSA, reflecting the renter income demographics and the scarcity of quality multifamily product.

Common Questions

Colorado Springs Multifamily — Frequently Asked Questions

What makes Colorado Springs a strong multifamily market?
Colorado Springs benefits from one of the highest concentrations of military employment in the nation — Fort Carson, Peterson Space Force Base, Schriever Space Force Base, and the Air Force Academy collectively anchor demand for workforce rental housing, providing recession-resistant occupancy.
What are typical cap rates for Colorado Springs apartments?
Colorado Springs cap rates typically range from 5.5% to 7.5% depending on location, vintage, and asset class. Military-adjacent workforce housing in established submarkets trades tighter; older value-add product further from employment nodes tends toward the higher end.
How does CO Multifamily Advisors market Colorado Springs assets?
We leverage our Denver-based Colliers platform to reach institutional value-add funds, regional private capital, and military-housing specialists — buyers a local-only brokerage may not reach. Competitive tension at offer time is the single largest driver of sale price.
