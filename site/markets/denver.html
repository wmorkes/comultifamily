The Denver Multifamily Market

Denver is consistently one of the top 10 most active multifamily investment markets in the United States. A diversified employment base anchored by technology, aerospace, defense, energy, and healthcare — combined with strong in-migration from California, the Midwest, and the Northeast — continues to drive apartment demand across the metro area. Denver's population has grown by more than 20% over the past decade, and household formation trends remain favorable despite short-term supply headwinds.

The market absorbed a significant wave of new supply between 2021 and 2025, particularly in the urban core submarkets of RiNo, LoDo, and the Central Business District. That supply pressure is now largely behind us. The development pipeline has thinned considerably as construction financing has tightened and entitlement timelines have lengthened. Absorption is improving, effective rents are stabilizing, and concessions are burning off across most submarkets — setting up a favorable environment for sellers in 2026 and beyond.

From an investment sales perspective, Denver attracts both local private capital and institutional buyers from across the country. That depth of demand is what creates the competitive tension that maximizes sale prices. We've represented sellers in Denver ranging from individual investors with a single 20-unit asset to institutional owners disposing of $100M+ portfolios — and the process we bring to every assignment is the same: disciplined underwriting, targeted buyer outreach, and relentless execution through close.

What Drives Denver Multifamily Value

Several structural factors support long-term apartment demand in the Denver metro. Employment diversity insulates the market against sector-specific downturns in a way that single-industry cities cannot replicate. The University of Colorado Denver, Metropolitan State University, and a growing community college system generate consistent household formation within the metro. Denver International Airport's expansion and the continued build-out of the RTD light rail network continue to reshape suburban demand patterns, opening new corridors for multifamily investment.

Affordability relative to coastal markets remains a draw for both residents and investors. Despite significant rent growth since 2015, Denver rents remain well below comparable supply-constrained coastal markets — which has historically attracted renters who would otherwise become homeowners, supporting occupancy rates across the rent spectrum.

Asset Types We Transact in Denver

Our Denver transactions span the full product spectrum. Value-add garden-style assets in Aurora, Westminster, and Lakewood represent our highest transaction volume — these assets attract a wide buyer pool ranging from high-net-worth individuals to institutional value-add funds. Urban infill product in RiNo, Cap Hill, and Uptown typically requires a more targeted buyer approach given the complexity of urban operating dynamics and the premium underwriting assumptions required to support pricing.

Affordable housing transactions in the Denver metro have become an increasingly significant part of our practice. LIHTC and Section 8 assets in suburban Denver submarkets attract mission-driven buyers, non-profit organizations, and specialized affordable housing funds — a distinct buyer pool that requires specialized relationships. Our Columbine Village sale in Arvada — a 232-unit senior LIHTC community that closed at $44.75M — is one example of this expertise applied in the Denver metro.

New construction lease-up assets require brokers with deep capital markets knowledge and strong institutional relationships. We've worked with developers disposing of newly stabilized product and understand how to position lease-up assets to the buyers best equipped to underwrite them.
